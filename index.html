Introducing Draconic

2022-11-22 4:42 pm

For the past 70 years, the consensus of historical linguistics has been that Vietnamese and Chinese are unrelated languages. That consensus is incorrect.

I have reconstructed the ancestor of Vietnamese and Chinese via the comparative method, proving that they are related.

Both the Vietnamese (Kinh) and the Chinese (Han) describe themselves as descendants of the dragon. Hence, I have dubbed this language family the Draconic languages. The Draconic languages are part of the Sino-Tibetan language family, which includes Tibetan and Burmese.

I believe other South-East Asian language families are also part of the Draconic language family, but that is beyond the scope of the current work, and I plan to explore it more in the future.

The Draconic Family Tree #

In the history of the Vietnamese language, I posit stages that I will call Proto-Draconic, Au Lac, and Linh Nam. I also make reference to a now-extinct Chinese dialect called Annamite Chinese, whose existence can be inferred from Sino-Vietnamese.

  • Proto-Draconic
    • Early Annamite Chinese
      • Late Annamite Chinese
    • Old Chinese
      • Proto-Min
        • Modern Min Chinese dialects
      • Middle Chinese
        • Proto-Mandarin
          • Mandarin dialects
        • Other Chinese varieties (e.g. Wu, Xiang, Gan, Yue)
    • Au Lac
      • Proto-Kinh-Muong
        • Early Kinh
          • Middle Kinh
            • Modern Kinh ("Vietnamese")
        • Muong languages

  Proto-Draconic (PD) is the reconstructed ancestor of Vietnamese and Chinese, which will be described on this website.

  Old Chinese is the ancestor of the languages that are today generally known as Chinese, including the Mandarin, Cantonese, Hakka, and Min languages.

The pronunciation of Proto-Han is uncertain, but numerous linguists have attempted to reconstruct it. I will often make reference to reconstructions of Zhengzhang Shangfang (ZS) and William Baxter & Laurent Sagart (BS). I generally follow Zhengzhang Shangfang's reconstruction. I will use the letters OC to indicate my own reconstruction, particularly where it differs from ZS.

  Proto-Min is the reconstructed ancestor of the Min Chinese languages, which include Taiwanese Hokkien and Amoy.

  Middle Chinese is the language represented by the Guangyun, an 11th Century rhyme dictionary commissioned by Emperor Zhenzong of the Song Dynasty. It is believed to represent the ancestor of all modern Chinese dialects except the Min languages. The exact phonetic values represented by Guangyun are uncertain.

Many linguists have attempted to reconstruct Middle Han. A deficiency of most reconstructions is that they fail to explain all aspects of the organization of the Guangyun, particularly the question of why some characters are placed in what is called the "third deng" or third grade. I explain this aspect of the Guangyun by associating the third deng with a Medial l that has been lost in all modern varieties of Chinese, but of which traces can be found in Sino-Vietnamese. I also associate the Guangyun term “closed” with a Medial w. My reconstruction of Middle Chinese is indicated with the letters MC.

  Annamite Chinese is an extinct Chinese language that was spoken by a Han minority in the Red River Delta (the region around Hanoi). Late Annamite Chinese is the main source of the Sino-Vietnamese readings of Han characters which are traditional in Vietnam. Linguist John D Phan (with whom I concur) posited its existence.

  Au Lac was the primary language spoken in the Red River Delta region (around Hanoi) before the region was absorbed into the Chinese kingdom of Nam Viet. It had a Japonic substrate and I suspect it was a creole.

  Proto-Kinh-Muong was the primary language spoken in the Red River Delta region after being absorbed into the kingdom of Nam Viet (南越). It differs from Au Lac in having absorbed a large amount of vocabulary from Early Annamite Chinese.

  Modern Kinh is the language of the Kinh (京) people, who are the majority ethnic group in Vietnam. It is the official language of Vietnam. It is usually called "Vietnamese", though it pre-dates the founding of the country of Vietnam. Middle Kinh is the language attested by Alexandre de Rhodes's Dictionarium Annamiticum Lusitanum et Latinum, published in 1651. Early Kinh is attested by early Chu Nom documents. Note that words may have entered Vietnamese via Au Lac, Annamite Chinese, or from the Japonic substrate.

  The Muong languages are spoken by the Muong people, who are the third-largest ethnic group of Vietnam.

Proto-Draconic Phonology #

The Proto-Draconic syllable had the following structure.

  • A pre-initial consonant (optional). One of:
    • m
    • s
  • An initial consonant. One of:
    • ʔ h ɦ
    • t tʰ d n̥ n̥ʰ n
    • c cʰ ɟ ɲ̊ ɲ̊ʰ ɲ
    • p pʰ b m̥ m̥ʰ m
    • k kʰ ɡ ŋ̊ ŋ̊ʰ ŋ
    • s sʰ z
    • ts tsʰ dz
    • ks ksʰ gz
    • w̥ wʰ w
    • l
    • r
  • One or more medial consonants (optional):
    • r l w
  • A vowel. One of:
    • uː oː iː eː
    • u o i e
  • A final consonant (optional). One of:
    • m n ɲ ŋ
    • p t c k
    • b d ɡ
    • w wɢ
    • j l
  • Post-final s (optional)

Proto-Draconic did not have a Medial j, but a medial j did develop in Old Chinese and Early Annamite Chinese, leading to a distinction between Type A and Type B syllables.

A Note About Notation #

The pronunciations of Vietnamese and Annamite Chinese will be given in a modified form of Quoc Ngu (Vietnam’s standard orthography). The modifications are:

  • The consonantal sound normally represented by the digraph GI will be instead written as J. This is necessary to avoid ambiguities that exist in the standard orthography.
  • The semi-vocalic glide usually represented by O or U is here represented by W, to avoid confusion with the nuclear vowels O and U.
  • For simplicity, the initial consonantal sound represented by K, C, or Q is consistently written as K.
  • For consistency, a final Y or I is consistently written as Y.

The six tones of Vietnamese and Muong can be analyzed as consisting of a height ("high" and "low") and a contour ("rising", "falling", or "level"). (These terms are etymological and do not reflect how the tones of Vietnamese and Muong are pronounced today.) I will use an acute accent (´) to represent so-called "high" tones (ngang, sac, hoi) and a grave accent (`) for so-called "low" tones (huyen, nang, nga). The rising tones (sac and nang) will be indicated by ↗︎ and the falling tones (hoi and nga) will be indicated by ↘︎.

Vowels

2022-11-22 5:42 pm

ZS and BS both reconstruct Old Chinese as having six vowel qualities. They both have a, e, i, o, u. For the sixth vowel, ZS has ɯ while BS has ə.

In addition, ZS reconstructs a length distinction for Old Chinese, so that each vowel quality can be long or short, for a total of 12 vowels (a, aː, e, eː, i, iː, o, oː, u, uː, ɯ, ɯː). In ZS's reconstruction, this is the source of the Type A/Type B distinction in Middle Chinese. That is, Old Chinese syllables with long vowels became Type A syllables in Middle Chinese, while Old Chinese syllables with short vowels became Type B syllables.

BS instead reconstruct syllables as being pharyngealized or non-pharyngealized. They believe Old Chinese pharyngealized syllables became Type A syllables in Middle Chinese, while Old Chinese non-pharyngealized syllables became Type B syllables in Middle Chinese.

I agree with ZS's vowel phonology for Old Chinese. However, I do not project this vowel system back to Proto-Draconic. I believe Proto-Draconic had only four vowel qualities (e, i, o, u) with a length distinction, for a total of eight vowels (e, eː, i, iː, o, oː, u, uː).

I believe Proto-Draconic front vowels (e, eː, i, iː) centralized in Old Chinese (producing respectively ɯ, jaː, ɯ, ɯː), except when preceded by medial l (or rl). As a result of this shift, all front-vowels in Old Chinese were preceded by a medial l. This medial l persisted into Guangyun Chinese before long vowels, which is why words that ZS reconstructs as having long front vowels (eː, iː) in Old Chinese are generally in the fourth-grade of the Guangyun (the fourth grade representing medial l). However, words with Proto-Draconic rl ended up in the second grade of the Guangyun, as PD rl shifted to r in Guangyun Chinese (the second grade representing medial r). Furthermore, words with short front vowels in Old Chinese (e, i) lost the medial l and developed a medial j, which is why those words are placed in the third grade of the Guangyun (the third grade representing medial j).

I also believe Proto-Draconic back vowels (o, oː, u, uː) centralized to Old Chinese (a, aː, ɯ, ɯː), except when preceded by medial w. As a result of this shift, all back vowels in Old Chinese were preceded by a medial w. This medial w persisted into Guangyun Chinese, which is why words that ZS reconstructs as having back vowels in Old Chinese are generally classed as "closed" in the Guangyun ("closed" representing a medial w).

Medial Consonants

2022-11-22 2:06 pm

BS & ZS #

Medial consonants are one area where the BS and ZS reconstructions of Old Chinese differ.

ZS reconstructs Old Chinese as having four medial consonants: w r l j. In ZS's reconstruction, medial r and medial l cannot occur together in the same word. Also, medial r and medial j cannot occur together in the same word. ZS's reconstruction of qʰwljilʔ for 水 'water' is an example of a word with three medial consonants.

[Actually, ZS hesitates. Sometimes he treats w as a medial consonant, which only occurs after velars and uvulars (kw, kʰw, ɡw, ŋw, qw, qʰw, ɢw). At other times, he reconstructs series of labialized velars and labialized uvulars (kʷ, kʰʷ, ɡʷ, ŋʷ, qʷ, qʰʷ, ɢʷ). They are effectively equivalent. On this website, I will treat w as a medial consonant in ZS's reconstructions.]

Interestingly, ZS's medial l is not reconstructed based on phonetic evidence from modern Chinese dialects, but is instead reconstructed based on graphical evidence from Chinese writing. For example, if two Chinese words are written with the same phonetic component, and one word can be reconstructed as ɡam while the other can be reconstructed as lam, ZS figures that the common phonetic component may have been *ɡlam.

BS reconstructs Old Chinese as having only one medial consonant: r. Where ZS has a medial w, BS has a labialized velar or uvular consonant. Where ZS has a medial l, BS has nothing. (ZS's medial j has unpredictable correspondences in BS.)

My reconstruction #

My reconstruction of Proto-Draconic has three medial consonants: r l w. Medial r and medial l can co-occur as rl. Medial l and medial w can co-occur as lw. Medial r and medial w can co-occur as rw. (I'd guess maybe all three can co-occur as rlw, but I don't have any examples.) This can be summarised as [r][l][w].

In Old Chinese, PD rl simplified to r. So the possible medial clusters were r, l, w, lw, and rw. This can be summarised as [r|l][w].

In Early Middle Chinese, a medial j developed before short vowels. This situation can be summarized as [r|l][w][j]. Then, through metathesis, the w moved before the other medials. This situation can be summarized as [w][r|l][j]. However, j was lost after r, and l was lost before j. So j could not co-occur with r or l. [I'm not actually sure of the order of these changes. It doesn't matter, though.]

This resulted in a situation that be summarized as [w][r|l|j], which is the situation of Guangyun Chinese. The Guangyun term "closed" or "open" represented the presence or absence of a w. The Guangyun grades represented the presence of r, l, or j. (The second grade represented medial r. The third grade represented medial j. The fourth grade represented medial l. The first grade represented the absence of r, l, or j.)

Then, in Late Middle Chinese, medial l was completely lost. This resulted in a situation which can be described as [w][r|j]. This is the situation reflected in many linguists' reconstruction of Middle Chinese.
